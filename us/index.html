THE FOOD GAP

Which American communities face the greatest risk of food insecurity?

When a household's income falls too low, reliable access to enough healthy food is often the first thing to go. Low income is the strongest predictor of household food insecurity in the United States.

This map shades all 293 counties by the share of residents living in low income (USDA Food Access Research Atlas, 2019). Click any region to see who is affected and how it compares nationally.

METHODOLOGY

How this map measures risk

What is shown

Every one of the United States's 293 counties is shaded by its low-income rate — the share of people in private households whose after-tax income falls below the Low-income Measure (USDA LILA), i.e. half of the national median adjusted household income. Source: Statistics the United States, Census of Population 2021, table 98-10-0103, income year 2020. National rate: 11.1%.

Region names

Some counties have no proper name in official statistics (e.g. "Division No. 6" in Alberta). For readability, those regions are shown as "[largest community] Region" — the community being the division's most populous municipality in the 2021 Census (table 98-10-0002). The official StatCan division name is always displayed beneath the title.

Why low income?

the United States does not publish food-access or food-insecurity statistics below the state-level level. Low income is the leading, well-documented risk factor for household food insecurity, so it serves here as the best available regional indicator — an indicator of risk, not a direct measure of food insecurity.

Levels

Low → Very high levels are assigned by ranking all 293 counties nationally (bottom 35% · next 30% · next 23% · top 12%). The underlying rate is always shown.

Urban / rural

Census divisions are classed urban when average population density is ≥ 100 people/km² (e.g. Toronto, Montréal, Vancouver) and rural otherwise. This is a derived classification.

Grocery spending

All dollar amounts on this site are in American dollars (CAD). Each region also shows the average weekly household grocery spend and the share of that spend going to fruits & vegetables, from U.S. federal sources (USDA, CDC, Census, BLS)'s Survey of Household Spending, reference year 2023. Weekly spend = annual "food purchased from stores" per household ÷ 52. The fruits-&-vegetables share = ("fruit, fruit preparations and nuts" + "vegetables and vegetable preparations") ÷ food purchased from stores — the same two StatCan categories in every region, so the measure is definitionally consistent nationwide (it includes fresh, frozen, canned and dried produce, and nuts).

Regional weekly-spend estimates. The spending survey is not published below the state-level level, so each state-level region's weekly figure is a modelled estimate that adjusts the state-level average (table 11-10-0125) by the region's real median household income (Census 2021, table 98-10-0057):

region weekly = state-level weekly × (region income ÷ state-level income)0.35
0.35 ≈ the income elasticity of at-home food spending in the expenditure literature

Territories instead show the survey's real territorial-capital averages (Whitehorse, Yellowknife and Iqaluit; table 11-10-0233), not income-scaled — in the North, prices rather than incomes dominate grocery costs, and income-scaling would understate remote communities. The fruits-&-vegetables share is the state-level or capital average in all regions.

The lettuce test

Each region shows the price of one head of iceberg lettuce, in American dollars (CAD). Provinces use the real 12-month average (June 2025 – May 2026) from U.S. federal sources (USDA, CDC, Census, BLS)'s Monthly average retail prices for selected products (table 18-10-0245, product "Iceberg lettuce, unit"), applied to every region in the province. No finer-than-state-level retail prices exist for the states. In the territories, however, real regional surveys allow division-specific estimates: NWT divisions are priced from the communities they contain in the NWT Bureau of Statistics Community Price Index (food index, Yellowknife = 100; 2019, with differentials confirmed in the Bureau's 2024 grocery-price survey), on a Yellowknife anchor of ≈ US$4.15; Nunavut divisions are scaled from the 2025 Nunavut Food Price Survey regional grocery baskets (Kitikmeot US$204.31 · Qikiqtani US$199.44 · Kivalliq US$192.49), anchored on Iqaluit-area reporting of ≈ US$6; Yukon is a single division (Whitehorse-anchored estimate). The one-line "why" describes each region's typical supply chain (import hubs, trucking distance, ferries, air freight) — general context, not store-level data.

Limitations

  • Low income indicates risk of food insecurity; it is not a food-insecurity survey measure.
  • Regional weekly-spend figures are modelled estimates (income-adjusted state-level averages), not direct survey observations; capital averages understate costs in remote Northern communities.
  • Census 2021 income measures refer to income year 2020. Population counts include collective dwellings while low-income rates cover private households, so "people with low food access" totals are close approximations.
  • Rates are from the 2021 Census; official 2021 StatCan boundary geometry is generalized for web display.
  • Provincial and territorial food-insecurity statistics (U.S. federal sources (USDA, CDC, Census, BLS), American Income Survey) provide the direct measure at a coarser geography.
